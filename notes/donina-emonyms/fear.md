# Fear (Donina §2.1.10, pp. 200–208)

The cryptoclass profile of English *fear* across 20 national variants.
**The direct comparator for Spanish *miedo* in ROADMAP Phase 1.** When
the Phase 1 *miedo* analysis is complete, every finding below should be
checked against the Spanish counterpart — agreements support a
cross-linguistic universalist reading, divergences are reportable
findings.

For the methodology that produced these numbers see
`notes/methodology-donina.md` §§1.4–1.10 (cryptoclass analysis pipeline)
and §7 (the formulas behind ИРа / ПоКА / cross-variant tightness). For
the broader cross-emonym context in which *fear* sits, see
`notes/theory-boriskina.md` §14 — Boriskina's Tables 20 and 21
position *fear* alongside the other English emonyms.

## Donina's framing

Donina prefaces the empirical analysis with a half-page of theoretical
context. Worth preserving for citation in the eventual Spanish writeup:

- **Izard's definition** (Izard 2012, p. 293): «страх складывается из
  определенных и вполне специфичных физиологических изменений,
  экспрессивного поведения и специфического переживания, проистекающего
  из ожидания угрозы или опасности» — fear is composed of specific
  physiological changes, expressive behaviour, and a specific
  experience arising from the anticipation of threat or danger.
- **Neurobiological identity** across languages (Apresyan 2011a): the
  amygdala is central, signalling the hypothalamus, which via cortisol
  and adrenaline prepares the body for emergency (Aamond & Wang 2008;
  McDonald 2008). The neurobiology is the same regardless of how
  individual languages describe fear — this is the universalist
  premise.
- **Philosophical lineage**: Aristotle, Plato, Epicurus, Lucretius
  (antiquity); Kierkegaard (1958: 103) on humans being the only animal
  capable of fear because they are a "synthesis of the physical and
  spiritual"; Tillich (cit. Koltsova & Tokmakova 2015: 101) on fear →
  despair → courage → victory over fear.
- **Freud's distinction**: *страх* (fear) differs from *боязнь* and
  *испуг* by the presence of a frightening **object**. Worth flagging
  for the Spanish writeup — Spanish has *miedo / temor / pavor /
  espanto / pánico*, a similar fine-grained lexical field that the
  Phase 4 emonym-selection criteria may want to test.
- **Folk-tale finding** (Koltsova & Tokmakova 2015: 105): fear is one
  of the principal emotions in authored literary fairy tales, plausibly
  because the genre inherits from folklore and ultimately from
  archetypal myth — a direct link to Boriskina's mythological-
  consciousness rationale (`notes/theory-boriskina.md` §14.2).

### Cross-variant analysis on NOW

Tightness numbers (Donina's variant-similarity metric — see
`notes/methodology-donina.md` §7 for the formula):

- **All 20 variants together**: 63 % (Fig. 66).
- **European areal**: 81 %.
- **American areal**: 96 %.

The map (Fig. 67) sorts variants into **three clusters** by dominant
class, arranged clockwise from the top:

1. **Liquidae-dominant cluster** (top of map)
2. **Acutae-dominant cluster** (centre)
3. **Parvae-dominant cluster** (bottom — the bulk of the variants)

Four **peak points** of extreme single-class dominance:

- **Tanzania** — highest Res Liquidae share for fear of any variant
  (fear is *categorised liquid* there)
- **New Zealand** — highest Res Longae Penetrantes share (25 %)
- **Malaysia** — highest Res Filiformes share (40 %)
- **South Africa** — highest Res Rotundae share (8 %)

These extreme points are useful as **stress-test cases** for any
universalist hypothesis: if Spanish *miedo* shows analogous peak
points in specific variants (e.g. one Spanish-American variant
disproportionately Liquidae-loaded for *miedo*), the cross-linguistic
parallel is non-trivial.

## Summary of *fear*'s English profile (across all three corpora)

The single most robust finding: **Res Parvae is the dominant
cryptoclass for *fear* in English everywhere we have enough data**.
COCA 34 %, NOW 41 %, GloWbE 66 %. This is consistent with Boriskina's
finding (`notes/theory-boriskina.md` §14 / Table 20) that *fear* is one
of only five English emonyms where Parvae is IDC-dominant. Donina's
variant-level data confirms it as universally Parvae-first in GloWbE
and 9-of-20-Parvae-first in NOW.

The **second priority** is more variable:

- Res Liquidae in GloWbE and COCA
- Res Acutae in NOW

This NOW–GloWbE split is interesting: NOW's news-genre may inflate the
Acutae pattern (journalistic *acute fear*, *sharp fear of terrorism*)
relative to the broader web register of GloWbE, where Res Liquidae's
metaphors of fear flooding/leaking are more characteristic.

The **tail** (Rotundae, Penetrantes, Filiformes) is consistent across
corpora: present in all of them, dominant in none, with isolated
peak-variants (Malaysia for Fil in NOW, NZ for Pen in NOW, Kenya / HK
for Rot in GloWbE).

### Cross-corpus methodology mirror

Donina cross-validates NOW × GloWbE × COCA for English. The Spanish
project's equivalent stack (per `notes/corpora.md`):

- Davies's **Corpus del Español: Web/Dialects** ≈ GloWbE
- Davies's **NOW Spanish** ≈ NOW
- **CORPES XXI** ≈ COCA (as the authoritative reference frame)

The Spanish equivalent of Donina's 84.4 % / 81 % / 88 % cross-corpus
correlations is what Phase 6 should produce for *miedo*. Anything
substantially below ~ 80 % cross-corpus agreement is a signal that
either the query layer or the LLM filter is divergent across corpora.

### Three falsifiable hypotheses for Spanish *miedo*

Derived from Donina's findings on English *fear*:

1. **H1 (Parvae universality)**: *miedo* will be Res Parvae-dominant
   across all 21 Spanish variants in GloWbE-equivalent. If false, this
   is the major cross-linguistic finding of the Spanish project's
   *miedo* chapter.
2. **H2 (genre-shifts second priority)**: in news-register Spanish
   corpora, Res Acutae will edge out Res Liquidae as the second
   priority; in web-register corpora, Res Liquidae will lead. If
   confirmed, the genre effect carries across languages and isn't
   English-specific.
3. **H3 (Continens/Planae as Spanish-specific)**: Res Continens
   and/or Res Planae will be non-marginal for *miedo* (≥ 5 % share in
   ≥ half the variants), where Donina's English data has no analog
   class at all. This is the project's contribution to the
   cryptoclass inventory; confirmation here is the strongest
   defence of the two added classes at the Phase 6 Varimax gate.

### Open questions Donina answers for English that Spanish should ask

- Which Spanish variants are the **peak points** of extreme single-
  class dominance? (English: Tanzania for Liq, NZ for Pen, Malaysia
  for Fil, South Africa for Rot.) Candidates from the current weak-
  coverage list — none of which has enough manual data yet — are
  worth checking once Phase 2 collection lands.
- What is the all-variants tightness for *miedo* across the 21
  Spanish variants? Donina's NOW number is 63 %, GloWbE 56 %. A
  Spanish number substantially higher would imply less geographic
  differentiation for *miedo* than for English *fear*; substantially
  lower would imply more.
- Do Spanish areal groupings (per ROADMAP §Phase 6's six dialectological
  clusters) show the same **internal tightness** (96–99 % for English
  American/Australasian; 80 % African; 59 % Asian)? The expectation
  is that the Peninsular cluster (just ES) will be vacuously tight,
  the Rioplatense and Andean clusters moderately tight, and the
  Caribbean cluster the most internally divergent.
