# Theory — Zadobrivskaya (2019)

Extraction from Oksana Fyodorovna Zadobrivskaya (Задобривская Оксана
Фёдоровна), *Эмоции как «вместилище» в вариантах английского языка*
("Emotions as a 'container' in the variants of English"), kandidat
dissertation in Germanic languages (10.02.04), Voronezh State
University, 2019. Scientific advisor: O. O. Boriskina. Official
opponents: L. S. Abrosimova (Southern Federal University) and
E. V. Shustrova (Russian State Vocational Pedagogical University).
Leading organisation: Leningrad State University (A. S. Pushkin).
Defence: 2019-09-23, dissertation council Д 212.038.16 at VSU.

The source in `references/Avtoreferat_Zadobrivskaya_O_F.pdf` is the
**автореферат** — a 23-page condensed abstract distributed before the
defence — not the full dissertation (which is not currently in
`references/`; the VSU repository copy is at
http://www.science.vsu.ru/disser). The autoreferat is the canonical
source for the **structure, defended theses, and headline numbers**;
it does **not** carry the full per-emonym × per-variant tables, the
complete classifier-occurrence counts, or the appendix summary table.
Where those are needed, the full dissertation must be sourced
separately.

This is the **direct counterpart of `notes/theory-golikova.md`** for
the *other* of this project's two extra cryptoclasses. Golikova (2018)
established *Res Planae* as the **seventh** English cryptoclass;
Zadobrivskaya (2019) establishes ***Res Continens*** ("Вместилище",
container) as the **eighth**. Both are products of the same Voronezh
lexico-typological group (Kretov, Boriskina, Donina, Golikova,
Zadobrivskaya) and both predate or coincide with this project's
framing. Their combined effect is decisive for how the project should
describe its inventory — see §0.

For terminology, see `notes/glossary.md`. For the framework these notes
extend, see `notes/theory-boriskina.md`. For the per-class working
note, see `notes/cryptoclasses/res-continens.md`.

## 0 — Why this dissertation matters for the Spanish project

One correction, several reusable assets.

**The correction (decisive).** `CLAUDE.md` and `data/cryptoclasses.tsv`
described *Res Continens* and *Res Planae* as **Spanish-specific**
additions to Boriskina's six classes. Golikova 2018 already disproved
this for *Res Planae* (recorded in `notes/theory-golikova.md` §0).
Zadobrivskaya 2019 disproves it for *Res Continens*. The honest
chronology of the inventory is therefore:

- **Boriskina 2011** — 6 English cryptoclasses (Res Liquidae, Res
  Filiformes, Res Rotundae, Res Longae Penetrantes, Res Acutae, Res
  Parvae).
- **Donina 2016** — cross-variant method applied to those 6, over 23
  English emonyms × 20 variants. Adds no class.
- **Golikova 2018** — adds *Res Planae* (7th English class).
- **Zadobrivskaya 2019** — adds *Res Continens* (8th English class).
- **This project (Spanish)** — adopts the **full 8-class English
  inventory** and applies it cross-variant to Spanish.

The consequence is that **neither** of the project's two "extra"
classes is a Spanish innovation. They are simply the two classes
*beyond Boriskina's original six* that the project carries — both
already attested for English by the Voronezh group. The project's
contribution is the **cross-variant Spanish application**, not the
positing of new cryptoclasses. The repo-wide reframing this entails
(removing "Spanish-specific" labels) was carried out alongside this
extraction; see §8.

Zadobrivskaya's autoreferat states the standing inventory explicitly
(p. 5): at the time of her work *seven* English cryptoclasses were
described — Boriskina's six plus Golikova's *Res Planae* — and she adds
the eighth. Her count and the project's 8-class inventory coincide
exactly.

**The reusable assets.** Zadobrivskaya supplies, ready-made:

1. A complete **classifier inventory for *Res Continens*** in English
   (verbal / predicative / attributive), §4 — the calibrating template
   for the Spanish *recipiente* inventory.
2. A **typological warrant** for the [+container] feature: overt,
   morphologically-marked container classes in Russian (*-ница*), Avar,
   Navajo, Atsugewi, and Upper Kuskokwim, §3.
3. A **full cryptoclass portrait of *fear*** (Res Continens 71.51 %
   dominant), §5.2 — the direct cross-linguistic benchmark for Spanish
   *miedo*.
4. Two **cognitive-modelling principles** — *mutual reversibility* and
   the *matryoshka* (nesting) principle, §6 — both testable in Spanish.
5. A **per-emonym dominant-class table** for all 23 English emonyms,
   §5.4 — the comparator for the Spanish emonyms in scope.

## 1 — Object, subject, goal, hypothesis

### 1.1 — Object

**Object** (p. 6): the **23 English emonyms** — the *same set* as Donina
2016:

> *emotion, feeling, fear, terror, distress, anxiety, grief, pleasure,
> joy, happiness, surprise, passion, hope, pride, shame, guilt, relief,
> pity, sympathy, concern, interest, anger, love.*

The set was derived from a 180-item list of emotion names (Arnold,
Ekman, Friesen, Ortony, Plutchik, Schaver, …), filtered to those whose
dictionary definitions contain *feeling* or *emotion* (Oxford Learner's
/ Oxford English Dictionary), and restricted to the top two frequency
bands (***** and ****) in the Collins Cobuild English Dictionary.

**Emonym** is taken (after A. T. Khrolenko) as a noun naming a human
emotional-sensory experience (*fear, anger, passion*). Zadobrivskaya
treats *чувства* (feelings) and *эмоции* (emotions) as a single space
of эмоционально-чувственные переживания, both denoted by emonyms.

### 1.2 — Subject

**Subject** (p. 7): the **metaphorical combinability** of those emonyms
— their occurrence in verbal, predicative, and attributive
constructions with feature-words whose semantics points to the
**placement or containment of one object in another**, or to an object
having clearly delineated contours and a functionally-significant
**cavity**.

The cryptoclass is a metaphorical extension of a language category
whose nucleus is the set of object-names forming the **topotype
"container"**. The prototype names (имена-эталоны, p. 7):

> *cup, glass, box, bowl, bucket, basket.*

### 1.3 — Variants and corpus

**20 variants of English** (p. 6), with the codes used in
English-language scholarship:

> US, CA, GB, IE, AU, NZ, IN, LK, PK, BD, SG, MY, PH, HK, ZA, NG, GH,
> KE, TZ, JM.

(American, Canadian, British, Irish, Australian, New Zealand, Indian,
Sri Lankan, Pakistani, Bangladeshi, Singaporean, Malaysian, Filipino,
Hong Kong, South African, Nigerian, Ghanaian, Kenyan, Tanzanian,
Jamaican.)

**Research corpus**: **26,312 word-uses** of the emonyms (p. 7), drawn
from:

| Source | Size (autoreferat) | URL |
|---|---|---|
| NOW — News on the Web | 6+ bn words | http://corpus.byu.edu/now |
| GloWbE — Global Web-Based English | 1.9 bn | http://corpus.byu.edu/glowbe |
| COCA — Contemporary American English | 560 m | http://corpus.byu.edu/coca/ |
| iWeb — The Intelligent Web-Based Corpus | 14 bn | https://corpus.byu.edu/iweb |

Plus the project's own resource **COEL** — *Криптоклассы английского
языка* — at http://www.rgph.vsu.ru/coel, and etymological/explanatory
dictionaries of English.

### 1.4 — Goal, hypothesis, tasks

**Goal** (p. 7): to describe the metaphorical categorisation of
emotional-sensory experiences **as containers** across the 20 variants
of English.

**Hypothesis** (p. 6): emotional-sensory experiences are conceived and
metaphorised in English **by analogy with a container**.

**Tasks** (p. 7–8), paraphrased: (1) review approaches to the basic
form / cognitive model "container"; (2) give a **typological
justification** for the *Res Continens* cryptoclass against the
backdrop of grammatically-expressed noun classes in the world's
languages; (3) determine the classifying semantic feature; (4) form the
**classifier set** diagnosing emonym membership; (5) delimit the
object; (6) run qualitative and quantitative analysis of emonym
membership by variant; (7) deposit the data on **COEL**; (8) establish
regularities in the categorisation of emotions as containers via the
common and the variant-specific.

**Methods** (p. 8): general-scientific inductive method, linguistic
observation, corpus methods, descriptive method, lexico-syntactic and
semantic analysis of classifiers, distributional analysis of names,
contextual analysis, quantitative methods, cryptoclass analysis. (Like
Golikova, no Pearson / Kendall / Varimax / SOM — the heavy
cross-variant statistics remain Donina's instruments; Zadobrivskaya's
cross-variant layer is frequency-and-presence, not factor analysis.)

## 2 — The categorial feature and the topotype "container"

**Defended thesis 1** (p. 8):

> *В основании именного криптокласса английского языка «Res Continens»
> («Вместилище») лежит категориальный семантический признак ‘полый
> предмет стабильной формы, предназначенный для какого-либо
> содержимого’…*

The categorial feature is **‘a hollow object of stable form, intended
for some content’** (полый предмет стабильной формы, предназначенный
для какого-либо содержимого).

**Defended thesis 2** (p. 8): the classifier set reflects the
cognitively-significant properties of the topotype "container", each
classifier diagnosing membership on one (or several) of them:

- **three-dimensional stable form** (трёхмерная стабильная форма);
- **a cavity** for placing, storing and removing objects or substances
  — the functionally-significant property of the form (полость…);
- **fillability** (наполняемость), bounded by an empty / full cavity.

The theoretical grounding (Ch. 1) is the integration with the
Moscow lexico-typological group's (MLexT: Rakhilina, Lyashevskaya,
Reznikova, Kashkin, Ryzhova) **topotype** concept and Kubryakova's
**image schema "container"** — the same topotype linkage Golikova draws
for *Res Planae*, here applied to containment. See
[[topotype]] and Golikova §2.

## 3 — The typological warrant for Res Continens

Ch. 2 opens with the **typological principle** of isolating the class
(p. 14). By the group's (Kretov's) criterion, a cryptoclass is warranted
only if the categorial feature is **overtly grammaticalised in at least
one language**. Zadobrivskaya cites five:

| Language | Overt container marker |
|---|---|
| **Russian** | suffix **-ница** with "container" semantics: *чернильница* (inkwell), *мыльница* (soap-dish), *сахарница* (sugar-bowl), *пепельница* (ashtray), *перечница* (pepper-pot). |
| **Avar** | a **local case** meaning 'being inside an object that has an internal cavity which can be a container', e.g. *qanssinib* 'in the chest/trunk'. |
| **Navajo** | names denoting 'a container with hard edges, with content' are marked by the classifier **khǫ**. |
| **Atsugewi** | suffixes indicating the placement of an object into a particular container — analogues of the preposition *into*. |
| **Upper Kuskokwim** (Athabaskan, Alaska) | a grammatical class 'container of **stable** form' **and** a grammatical class 'container of **unstable** form' (e.g. a sack); the classifier function is carried by **verbal stems**. |

English does covertly (via constructions) what these languages do
morphologically, satisfying the typological criterion for an 8th
covert class.

### 3.1 — For Spanish

The warrant transfers directly. **Spanish strengthens it from within
its own morphology**: the productive container suffixes **-ero / -era**
(*azucarero* sugar-bowl, *salero* salt-cellar, *tintero* inkwell,
*cenicero* ashtray, *pimentero* pepper-pot) are the exact functional
cognates of Russian *-ница*. This is a same-family overt-classifier
echo Zadobrivskaya could not cite (her object is English) but the
Spanish project can and should, *in addition to* reusing her five
languages. The Spanish *Res Continens* therefore has a **stronger**
morphological typological footing than the English one.

## 4 — The Res Continens classifier inventory (English)

**Defended thesis 3** (p. 8–9). Three construction types.

### 4.1 — Verbal classifiers (глагольные)

The dominant group. Sub-divided by what they diagnose:

- **Subject classifier**: `[a container contains]`.
- **Object classifiers**: `[to fill a container (with smth.)]`,
  `[to empty a container]`.
- **Locative classifiers**, split by direction:
  - **Placement into the container** — transitive `[VO in/into a
    container]` (`[put/place smth in(to) a container]`, `[throw smth
    in(to) a container]`) and intransitive `[V in/into a container]`
    (`[fall, get in/into a container]`).
  - **Presence inside the container** — intransitive `[SVexist
    in/inside a container]` (`[be/exist/lie in/inside a container]`,
    `[there VS in a container]`) and transitive `[find smth in a
    container]`, `[have smth in a container]`, `[keep/hold smth in a
    container]`.
  - **Removal from the container** — transitive `[VO out of a
    container]` (`[take/get smth out of a container]`, `[pull smth out
    of a container]`) and intransitive `[V out of a container]`.

### 4.2 — Predicative classifiers (предикативные)

`[a container is full (of smth.)]`, `[a container is filled (with
smth.)]`, `[a container is empty]`, `[a container is deep]`, `[a
container is shallow]`.

### 4.3 — Attributive classifiers (атрибутивные)

The pair `[a full container]`, `[an empty container]`.

### 4.4 — Construction-type shares

**Defended thesis 4** (p. 9), Рис. 1 (p. 15):

| Construction type | Share |
|---|---|
| **Verbal** (locative dominant) | **95.25 %** |
| Attributive | 3.37 % |
| Predicative | 1.39 % |

The verbal locative constructions mark the **regularity and
conventionality** ("cognitive recognition" in the speech community) of
the feature ascribed to the emonym. The most functionally-significant
and communicatively-demanded feature across the anglosphere is the
**existential** one — 'presence of content inside the cavity',
construction `[SVexist in/inside a container]` (`fear is in …`, `there
is X in fear`). Predication, though rare (1.39 %), is the **favourable
environment for linguistic creativity** — the forge of new metaphorical
images (cf. Golikova's Kustova-based account of predicative
constructions as a "laboratory" of new senses, `theory-golikova.md`
§5.1).

### 4.5 — For Spanish

The inventory maps onto Spanish almost slot-for-slot:

| English classifier | Spanish analogue |
|---|---|
| `[to fill a container]` / `[to empty …]` | *llenar / vaciar de + N* |
| `[put/place/throw smth into a container]` | *meter / poner / echar … en + N* |
| `[SVexist in/inside a container]` | *estar / haber … en + N*, *llevar dentro* |
| `[take/pull smth out of a container]` | *sacar / arrancar … de + N* |
| `[a container is full / empty]` | *N está lleno / vacío* |
| `[a container is deep / shallow]` | *un N profundo* |
| `[a full / an empty container]` | *un N lleno / vacío* |

Two concrete predictions the pipeline can test: (a) the **verbal
locative dominance** (~95 %) should replicate, with *estar/haber en*
and *meter/sacar de* dwarfing the predicative *está lleno/vacío*; and
(b) the **existential** construction (*hay X en el miedo*, *dentro del
miedo*) should be the single most frequent slot, as it is in English.

## 5 — Cryptoclass analysis of the emonyms

### 5.1 — All 23 are metaphoronyms of Res Continens

**Defended thesis 5** (p. 9). Every one of the 23 emonyms tested
positive as a metaphoronym of *Res Continens*. *fear* and *love* show
the highest co-occurrence frequency with the classifiers, **primarily
in the American variant**. Maximum co-occurrence frequency overall is
found for *pity*, *pride*, and *guilt* across all variants.

### 5.2 — The fear portrait (Рис. 2, p. 16)

*fear* is the most frequent emonym: **8,167 word-uses = 31.04 %** of
all emonym uses. Its cryptoclass portrait (per COEL):

| Cryptoclass | Share for *fear* |
|---|---|
| **Res Continens** | **71.51 %** |
| Res Planae | 10.73 % |
| Res Parvae | 6.08 % |
| Res Liquidae | 4.46 % |
| Res Filiformes | 2.37 % |
| Res Acutae | 2.18 % |
| Res Longae Penetrantes | 2.18 % |
| Res Rotundae | 0.47 % |

For the anglophone mind *fear* associates **first and overwhelmingly
with a container** — nearly **7× more** than with the flat image (*Res
Planae*), which comes second. Then, descending: graspable, liquid,
threadlike, sharp, penetrating, round.

**This recontextualises the earlier "fear = Res Acutae" expectation.**
`theory-golikova.md` §6.1 and the Donina emonym notes flagged *fear* as
Res-Acutae-led on the cryptoclass-chain figure (which excludes the two
newer classes). Once *Res Continens* and *Res Planae* are in the
inventory, *fear* is **container-dominant**, and Res Acutae drops to
2.18 %. The Spanish manual data — strong `nivel de miedo` (Res Planae)
plus container uses — is consistent with this: for *miedo* the leading
images should be **container then flat**, not sharp.

### 5.3 — fear by variant (Рис. 3, p. 17)

Absolute word-use counts for *fear* with *Res Continens* classifiers:

| GB | US | CA | IE | ZA | IN | NG | AU | NZ | PK | MY | KE | JM | GH | LK | SG | PH | BD | TZ | HK |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
|1603|1530|1006|688|637|589|418|358|197|170|135|123|98|74|73|71|70|43|36|7|

The existential `[be/exist in a container]` is usual (узуальное) in
nearly all variants; the fullness feature `[be in a container]` peaks
in the established GB / US / CA variants.

### 5.4 — Dominant cryptoclass per emonym (p. 19)

From the cryptoclass portraits of all 23 emonyms:

| Dominant class | Emonyms | n |
|---|---|---|
| **Res Continens** | *feeling, fear, terror, grief, surprise, shame, relief, sympathy, anger, love* | 10 |
| **Res Planae** | *emotion, happiness, passion, guilt, concern, interest* | 6 |
| **Res Parvae** | *pleasure, joy, hope, pride, pity* | 5 |
| *(no portrait)* | *distress, anxiety* — no data for the other seven classes | 2 |

So **container is the dominant image for the larger part of the
emonyms** (10 of the 21 with portraits), which underwrites **defended
thesis 8** (p. 10): the feature 'hollow object of stable form intended
for some content' dominates in **more than half** of the examined
emotions.

**Mapping to the Spanish emonyms in scope** (*miedo, tristeza, amor,
alegría, ira*):

| Spanish | Nearest English emonym(s) | Zadobrivskaya's dominant class |
|---|---|---|
| *miedo* | fear | **Res Continens** (71.51 %) |
| *tristeza* | grief / sadness | **Res Continens** (grief is container-dominant) |
| *amor* | love | **Res Continens** |
| *alegría* | joy / happiness | Res Parvae (joy) / Res Planae (happiness) — **split** |
| *ira* | anger | **Res Continens** |

Four of the five Spanish emonyms map to **container-dominant** English
emonyms; only *alegría* is ambiguous (its two English analogues split
between Res Parvae and Res Planae). This is a strong prior for the
Spanish study: *Res Continens* should be a leading — often the leading —
class for *miedo, tristeza, amor, ira*, which makes the project's
inclusion of the class essential rather than peripheral.

## 6 — Cognitive-modelling principles

**Defended thesis 7** (p. 9–10). Two regularities govern the
categorisation of emotions as containers, both special cases of the
mythological-thinking properties *протеизм* (proteism) and *синкретизм*
(syncretism):

### 6.1 — Mutual reversibility (принцип взаимной обратимости)

A special case of proteism: an emotion is categorised **both as the
containing and as the contained**. The same pair of emonyms can swap
roles across variants:

- *hope ⇄ joy*: in **Canadian**, hope is the container *filled with*
  joy (*filling hope with joy*, CA); in **Nigerian**, joy is the
  container from which hope is taken (*take hope out of the joy*, NG).
- The hyperonyms *emotion ⇄ feeling* reverse within a single variant
  (US): *your feelings are in your emotions* (US) and *put your emotions
  into your feelings* (US) — feelings are stored in emotions and
  emotions in feelings, evidence of their symbiosis.

Crucially, reversible pairs are **not** tied to a single variant or
areal zone — mutual reversibility is an **a-temporal, areally
unconditioned** principle of the categorisation.

### 6.2 — The matryoshka / nesting principle (принцип «матрёшки»)

Embeddedness of one emotion-image inside another, including images of
the **same** nature (*love within love*): in **Sri Lankan**, love is
filled with carnal love (ex. 21); in **Australian**, one person's
feeling is found inside another's (ex. 22); in **Indian**, a man's
happiness lies at the bottom of a woman's happiness (ex. 23).

The **largest nesting chain** assembled spans **14 emonyms** (more than
half the set, ex. 27):

> *love ⇄ hope ⇄ shame ⇄ pleasure ⇄ happiness ⇄ grief ⇄ anger ⇄
> emotion ⇄ feeling ⇄ fear ⇄ anxiety ⇄ concern ⇄ interest.*

Like mutual reversibility, the matryoshka principle has **no areal
binding**.

### 6.3 — Linguistic complementarity (p. 19)

A further principle (linguistic complementarity in the metaphorical
representation of emotions-as-containers): different variants mark
different properties of the same container. E.g. for *anger*: the
**Indian** variant foregrounds *fullness*; **Canadian / British / South
African** foreground *depth*; **Australian / NZ** foreground that the
cavity *can be empty*. Placement `[VO in/into a container]` is
especially significant only in **American**; removal `[VO out of a
container]` is significant in **American, NZ, Indian, South African,
Nigerian**.

### 6.4 — For Spanish

All three principles are concrete, testable predictions:

- **Reversibility**: do *esperanza* and *alegría* swap container roles
  across Spanish variants (*llenar la esperanza de alegría* vs.
  *sacar esperanza de la alegría*)?
- **Matryoshka**: *un amor dentro del amor*, *el miedo dentro del
  miedo* — does Spanish assemble comparable nesting chains, and are
  they likewise areally unbound across the 21 variants?
- **Complementarity**: which container property (*lleno* / *profundo* /
  *vacío*) does each Spanish variant foreground for *ira*, *miedo*?

The autoreferat's finding that reversibility and nesting are
**areally unbound** is a useful null hypothesis for the Spanish
cross-variant layer: if these principles are areally unbound in
English, the Spanish project should expect them *not* to discriminate
variants, and should not over-read areal signal into them.

## 7 — Open questions Zadobrivskaya leaves (Заключение, p. 22)

- Membership of the **500 frequent abstract English nouns** (the COEL
  platform set, the same pool Golikova analysed) in *Res Continens* —
  not yet computed.
- **3D visualisation** of the cryptoclass distribution.
- The classifying potential of the construction **`[feeling of …]`**,
  where the hyperonym *feeling* is the container and the emotion-name is
  the content — left for future work.
- **The container of *unstable* form** (a sack / мешок) is **untouched**.
  Zadobrivskaya notes the English cryptoclasses now form
  **stable/unstable dichotomies**: "long-thin stable form" vs.
  "long-thin unstable form" (*stick — rope*), and "flat stable form"
  vs. "flat unstable form" (*tabletop — tablecloth*). By analogy *Res
  Continens* (stable container) implies an as-yet-undescribed
  **unstable-container** class. This is a live extension point the
  Spanish project could note but need not pursue.

## 11 — One-paragraph summary for fast lookup

Zadobrivskaya (2019) establishes ***Res Continens*** ("Вместилище",
container) as the **eighth English cryptoclass**, categorising emonyms
by analogy with a **hollow object of stable form intended for some
content** (prototypes *cup, glass, box, bowl, bucket, basket*). The
class is typologically warranted by overt container markers elsewhere —
Russian *-ница*, Avar local case, Navajo *khǫ*, Atsugewi *into*-
suffixes, Upper Kuskokwim's stable/unstable container classes. The
English classifier inventory is verbal (95.25 %, locative-dominant:
placement *into* / presence *in* / removal *out of*), predicative
(1.39 %: *full / filled / empty / deep / shallow*), and attributive
(3.37 %: *a full / an empty container*); the existential *be in a
container* is the most communicatively demanded slot. All 23 English
emonyms (Donina's set) are metaphoronyms; **container is the dominant
image for the larger half** of them (*fear* 71.51 %, far above the
second-place *Res Planae* 10.73 %). Two areally-unbound cognitive
principles govern the categorisation — **mutual reversibility** (an
emotion is both container and content) and the **matryoshka** nesting
of one emotion-image inside another (a 14-emonym chain). For the Spanish
project the dissertation is decisive on framing — together with Golikova
2018 it shows that **neither *Res Continens* nor *Res Planae* is
Spanish-specific**; both are English-attested Voronezh-group classes,
and the project simply carries the **two extra classes beyond
Boriskina's six**. It also supplies the *recipiente* classifier
template, the typological warrant (strengthenable with Spanish
*-ero/-era*), the *miedo* benchmark (container-dominant, not sharp), and
three testable cognitive principles.
